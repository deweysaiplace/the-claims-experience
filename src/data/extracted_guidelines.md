### 1. Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for Wind & Hail Claims

#### **A. Pre-Inspection Procedures**
*   Obtain information from the policyholder regarding:
    *   The type of damage noted to the exterior, interior, or other structures.
    *   The number of stories and type of roofing material, if roof damage is reported.
    *   The extent of any interior damage (e.g., if damage is in one or multiple rooms).
*   Gather available information about the specific weather event.
*   Review policy information and any existing photos.

#### **B. Inspection Triggers & Guidelines**
*   **General:** A physical on-roof inspection should be completed when it is safe and practical to do so.
*   **Wind Damage:**
    *   Claims involving minor damage may be handled by phone.
    *   A physical inspection is necessary when:
        *   Damage is reported on multiple slopes.
        *   Exterior damage is extensive.
        *   The condition of the roof makes it more susceptible to damage.
*   **Hail Damage:**
    *   A physical inspection is generally necessary for all reported hail claims with potential damage.
    *   **PLR (Physical Loss Reporting) Inspection Workflow:** A physical on-roof inspection may not be required if the following conditions are met:
        *   Weather data confirms hail was 1.25" or larger.
        *   The initial estimate of collateral damage (to other parts of the property) is consistent with hail large enough to require a full roof replacement.

#### **C. Scope of Inspection (What to Inspect)**
*   **Exterior:**
    *   Conduct a complete walk-around of the property.
    *   Note damage to all parts of the building.
    *   Inspect and evaluate damage to other structures and appurtenances, including fences, outbuildings, swimming pools, and A/C units.
    *   Document the claim file with all findings.
*   **Interior:**
    *   If interior damage is reported, inspect the interior to evaluate if the damage is related to any damage on the roof surface.
*   **Roof:**
    *   Inspect the overall roof to establish its general condition and the extent of the damage.
    *   Consider the type of storm, the direction of the weather, and the construction of the residence.
    *   Inspect other roof-level items like satellite dishes, turbine vents, and A/C units to help establish if the damage is consistent with the event.

### Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for Wind/Hail Damage Claims (Roof)

*   **Initial Safety Assessment:**
    *   If a roof is damaged but deemed walkable, determine if walking on it will cause substantial further damage.
    *   In rare situations where it is unsafe to walk the roof, observe and photograph the damage from several points around the perimeter. Any questions regarding this approach should be reviewed with management.

*   **Inspection Safety:**
    *   All inspections must be conducted in accordance with the Workplace Safety Policy.
    *   Claim handlers must be able to complete the inspection/investigation, and the unsafe situation must be remedied before proceeding.
    *   If an unsafe situation arises (e.g., threats), leave the premises and allow law enforcement to handle it.
    *   Report any workplace violence threats via the Threat Report form or the 24/7 Threat Response Hotline.

*   **Inspection Process:**
    *   Document the type of roof, number of layers, pitch of the slopes, and any underlayment (e.g., ice and water barrier).
    *   Use a "test square" (typically 10'x10') to evaluate hail damage on all types of roof surfaces where safe and practical.
        *   Mark and count the number of damaged shingles within the square.
        *   Complete a test square on a number of slopes if damage is similar; if damage varies, complete a test square for each direction.
    *   For wind damage, test squares are not necessary unless damage is not obvious and they can help evaluate repairability.
    *   Inspect other appurtenances like vents, skylights, gutters, downspouts, flashing, and patio covers for damage from the covered peril.

### Rules Regarding Material Matching, Repair vs. Replacement, and Approvals

*   **Repair vs. Replacement Thresholds:**
    *   To determine if a roof or slope should be repaired or replaced, compare the cost to repair the actual damage to the cost to remove and replace the entire roof or slope.
    *   **50% Rule for Composition Shingle Roofs:** If the cost to repair actual damage on a composition roof or slope approaches or exceeds 50% of the cost to remove and replace that damaged slope, a replacement decision should be considered. This guideline also applies to other non-composition shingle/shake roofs.
    *   **Wind Damage:** For wind damage, the guideline is to apply this analysis to the specific "area of the wind damage." Consideration should be given to the extent of damage and whether the shingle can be repaired or replaced.
    *   **Hail Damage:** The repair vs. replace analysis should be used for hail losses where damage is found in test squares across the slope(s). Damage is determined by the number of damaged shingles found in the test square.

*   **Approvals (Management Review):**
    *   Management review is required for a full roof replacement recommendation under the following conditions:
        *   The weather event identifies wind gust speeds **less than or equal to 62 mph**.
        *   For hail claims, the Verisk 360 Hail Size Net Estimated at Loss Location indicates a hail size of **less than or equal to 1 inch**.
    *   The management review of the recommendation must be documented in the claim file.

*   **Material Matching:**
    *   The guidelines do not contain specific rules for material matching but emphasize documenting the "kind of materials and condition of the roof" in photos and notes.

### Wind and Water Damage SOPs

**Wind Damage to Composition Shingles**

*   **Sealant Strip:** Wind can break the heat-activated sealant strip on shingles. Once broken, this seal will not reseal.
    *   Claim handlers should note any unsealed shingles observed during the inspection.
    *   The file should include an analysis of unsealed shingles, observing for residue from the sealant on the shingle below as evidence of prior bonding.
*   **Hail vs. Wind:** Hail can cause granule loss, which may be confused with wind damage. Review these types of losses with the claim management.
*   **Mechanical Damage:** Differentiate from wind damage. Mechanical damage results from human activity (e.g., foot traffic, tools) and is often not random in nature. Some types of accidental mechanical damage may be covered.

**Wind Damage to Wood Shakes/Shingles**

*   **Damage Definition:** A wood shingle/shake is considered damaged by wind only when a split extends completely through the body of the shake and is exposed to the elements, impacting its water-shedding ability.
    *   Splits that are not exposed to the elements are not considered damage requiring repair/replacement.
*   **Splits:** Fresh splits from wind damage will typically show fresh, unweathered wood inside the split.
*   **Curling, Cupping, Cracking:** These are typically considered signs of aging or weathering, not direct accidental physical loss from wind.

**Footfall Damage**

*   Footfall can occur on all types of roof surfaces and may appear as cracks, granule loss, or broken/fractured shingles. This damage can be caused by walking on the roof.
*   If damage is caused by a claim handler during the inspection, it is included in the adjustment.
*   If there is a policy exclusion for footfall damage, and this is asserted as the cause of damage, the claim handler must document the basis for the denial for that portion of the damage.

### Material Matching, Repair vs. Replacement, and Approvals

**Repair vs. Replace Analysis (Composition Shingles)**

*   This analysis is used for adjusting spot losses where damage is scattered across a slope.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  Determine if the roof surface is repairable based on its composition and condition.
    2.  Count the total number of damaged shingles on the roof.
    3.  Apply the following **Damage Repair Factor** based on the age and condition of the shingles:
        *   **0 to 25% worn:** Factor of 1.0
        *   **25% to 75% worn:** Factor of 1.5
        *   **75%+ worn:** Factor of 2.0
    4.  Multiply the number of damaged shingles by the selected factor.
    5.  **Thresholds:**
        *   If the result is less than 20 shingles multiplied by 2 or 3, a 40-shingle repair is generally appropriate.
        *   If the cost to repair is within 25% of the cost to remove and replace a slope, replacement of the slope may be appropriate.

**Material Matching & Replacement**

*   **Delaminated Shingles:** Due to the difficulty in obtaining matching shingles, it may be necessary to replace three shingles for each one that needs to be replaced to address delamination. This applies to all roof types.
*   **Substituting Materials (e.g., Composition for Wood):**
    *   The estimate should first be prepared to provide like materials to repair/replace the existing wood roof.
    *   If the insured chooses to substitute (e.g., install a composition roof instead of the existing wood roof), the payment will be the lesser of the replacement cost of the wood roof or the cost to install the composition shingles.
    *   The Actual Cash Value (ACV) of the damage is based on the cost of repairing/replacing the *existing* wood roof.

### 1. Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for Wind & Hail Damage Claims (Roofing)

#### A. General Principles
*   **Covered Damage:**
    *   **Hail Damage:** Accidental direct physical loss to the roof decking surface, which may be covered. Hail damage may be observed when hailstones penetrate the decking, creating damage.
    *   **Wind Damage:** May be observed when wind causes all or a portion of the decking to be removed or unattached from the structure.
*   **Exclusions:** Pre-existing wear, tear, deterioration, or rot to a decking surface is not covered damage.
*   **Collateral Damage:** If removing or repairing covered damaged shingles causes unavoidable damage to the decking surface, an allowance to repair that damaged decking may be included as part of the covered repairs.
*   **Building Codes:** All claims should be reviewed individually with consideration of building codes, statutes, or regulations which may apply.

#### B. Composition Roofing Over Wood Shake/Shingle Decking
*   **Assessment:** Evaluate the extent of damage and repairability on an individual basis for both the composite shingle layer and the underlying wood shake/shingle layer (which is serving as decking).
*   **Repair Scenarios:**
    1.  **Composite Shingles Only:** If the composition shingles can be repaired without impacting the integrity of the underlying wood shake decking, no additional allowance for the decking is necessary.
    2.  **Collateral Damage to Decking:** If removing/repairing the composition shingles causes unavoidable damage to the wood decking, an allowance to repair the damaged decking may be included.
    3.  **Direct Damage to Decking:** If wind or hail directly damages the wood shake/shingle decking surface, the estimate should be for the removal of both the composite shingles and the wood shake/shingle decking. The estimate should then include the replacement of the damaged wood shake decking and one layer of composition shingles.
    *   **Note:** The installation of composite shingles over a wood shake/shingle decking surface is not recommended.

### 3. Material Matching, Repair vs. Replacement, and Approvals

#### A. Repair vs. Replacement Thresholds
*   **Roof Appurtenances:** It is generally not necessary to detach and replace undamaged items (e.g., satellite dishes, power vents, roof-mounted HVAC) unless the roof is being completely re-decked. Decisions should be handled on a case-by-case basis.
*   **Self-Adhered Waterproof Membrane (Ice & Water Barrier):**
    *   If the area with the membrane is damaged by a covered loss, the estimate should include the cost to replace the membrane.
    *   Removal of the membrane may cause decking to splinter. The standard shingle removal line items are expected to cover the labor for this, and a separate estimate for removal is not necessary.
    *   If sections of the membrane are not adhered, it may be necessary to separately estimate; contact your field trainer for guidance.

#### B. Material Matching
*   **Roof Appurtenances:** The policy provides coverage to replace certain undamaged items (e.g., satellite dishes, power vents, roof-mounted HVAC) to achieve a "reasonably uniform appearance" with the materials that are being estimated for replacement.
*   **Fascia/Soffit:** Replacement of undamaged components like fascia/soffit to achieve a match is to be evaluated based on individual circumstances.
*   **Depreciation:** Matching is considered a depreciable finishing item.

#### C. Approvals & Escalation
*   **Management Review:** Any questions regarding claims handling, building codes, or roof appurtenances not explicitly covered in the guidelines should be reviewed with management.

#### D. Estimating & Material Allowances
*   **Waste and Rounding (Composition Roofing):**
    *   Use the **Xactimate Auto Waste Calculator** to determine the proper waste percentage.
    *   If a sketch/roof bid is not available, use curved guidelines for calculating waste.
    *   Hip/ridge caps should be estimated separately from the field shingles.
*   **Waste and Rounding (Wood Roofing):**
    *   For hip/ridge caps (if waste is not already included in the price):
        *   **Hip roofs:** Use 15% waste.
        *   **Gable roofs:** Use 10% waste.
    *   After adding waste, round up to the next bundle.
*   **Waste and Rounding (Shingle Repairs - Single Slope/Not Entire Roof):**
    *   **Gable roofs:** Use 5% waste.
    *   **Hip roofs:** Use 10% waste.
*   **General Waste Rule:** Tear-off or removal line items should **not** include waste in the calculation.
*   **Steep and Two-Story Charges:**
    *   **Steep Charge (8:12 slope or greater):** Apply only to the specific slopes that require it. The "remove" activity charge applies to the area to be torn off (without waste). The "replace" activity charge applies to the total squares to install (with waste).
    *   **Two-Story Charge:** Should be included for every two-story roof slope. Follows the same remove/replace logic as the steep charge.
*   **Specialty Products:**
    *   **Starter Strips:** Estimate as a separate line item if not included in the waste percentage.
    *   **Hip and Ridge Caps:** Estimate as a separate line item if not included in the waste percentage.
    *   **Ice and Water Barrier:** Use the **"Ice and Water Barrier Calculator"** tool to calculate quantities.

### 1. Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for Wind Damage Claims

#### General Roof Claim Handling
*   **Tear-Off Policy:** When estimating for a roof surface replacement, the standard procedure is to tear off all existing layers of roofing.
*   **Expert/Engineer Usage:** The assistance of a roofing expert or engineer may be used to determine the cause of loss, conduct damage analysis, or evaluate estimates. The final decision on how to resolve a claim rests with the company.
*   **Subrogation:** If covered roof damage may have been caused by faulty roofing systems or products, a subrogation investigation may be necessary. This requires documenting the roof condition, identifying the products involved, and preserving evidence.
*   **Starter Strips:** When estimating for shingle damage, composition roofing starter strips should be considered a separate line item, as they are not included in the waste factor for shingles.

#### Special Considerations
*   **Solar Panels & Solar Water Heaters:**
    *   Handling the repair/replacement of shingles around or beneath solar panels and solar water heaters is done on a case-by-case basis.
    *   If there is sufficient room to work under the panels (i.e., the legs are tall enough), no detachment is necessary.
    *   If the panels must be detached and reset for shingle replacement, the policyholder should be advised to use a qualified person or property to handle the solar equipment.
    *   For solar water heaters, this process may also require the system to be drained, capped, re-pressurized, and put back into service.
*   **Valleys:**
    *   It is necessary to determine the material used in the valley construction (e.g., roll roofing, metal flashing).
    *   Consider any matching of materials when estimating.

#### Handling Claims with Prior, Unrepaired Damage
*   **Initial Step:** Before settling a current roof claim, it is important to review the insured's claim history for prior payments on the same property.
*   **General Guideline:** If a prior payment was made for damage that was not repaired, that damage is not recoverable again in a subsequent claim.
*   **Scenario A: Prior Claim Settled, No Repairs Made**
    1.  Adjust the new loss as a separate event, subject to the current policy deductible.
    2.  Apply new damages to the entire roof.
    3.  When calculating Actual Cash Value (ACV), consider the age and condition of the property, *including the prior unrepaired damage*.
    4.  Subtract the deductible for the new claim.
    5.  Deduct the amount of any prior payments for the roof damage that was not repaired.
    6.  If the new ACV payment exceeds the prior ACV payment, pay the policyholder the difference.
    7.  If the new ACV is the same or less than the prior ACV payment, no additional ACV is owed.
*   **Scenario B: Prior Claim Partially Repaired, New Loss Requires Full Replacement**
    1.  Adjust the loss as a separate event, subject to the policy deductible.
    2.  Estimate the full replacement of the roof based on the current date of loss price list.
    3.  Apply depreciation, considering the property's age and condition, including prior damage.
    4.  Subtract the deductible for the new claim.
    5.  Pay the insured the resulting ACV for the new claim.

### Wind & Roof Damage Claims Handling

#### **Handling Claims with Prior Unrepaired Damage**

This section outlines procedures for handling a new claim when a prior claim for the same area was not repaired.

*   **General Procedure for Subsequent Loss:**
    *   Adjust the new loss as a separate event, subject to its own policy deductible.
    *   Prepare a new estimate for the current loss using the price list for the current date of loss.
    *   When applying depreciation, the age and condition of the damaged property, **including the prior roof damage**, should be considered in determining the Actual Cash Value (ACV).
    *   Subtract the deductible for the new claim.

*   **Scenario: New Damage Occurs on a Roof with Unrepaired Prior Damage (Paid Claim)**
    *   If the new damage is distinct from the old damage but now requires the total replacement of the roof, the following amounts are subtracted from the new loss estimate:
        1.  The current deductible.
        2.  The payment amount from the prior claim.
        3.  Any amounts from the new estimate that overlap with damage estimated on the prior claim.
    *   If the prior claim was for a total loss and the time to claim replacement cost benefits has expired, the prior loss should be adjusted to its ACV. The new claim (if it independently requires total replacement) should be adjusted on a replacement cost basis.

*   **Scenario: Insured Took Reasonable Steps to Mitigate Prior Damage**
    *   If a subsequent event causes damage before repairs from a prior event are complete, but the insured took reasonable steps to mitigate and arrange for repairs, the claims should be handled as separate events.
    *   Evaluate each claim individually on its own merits.
    *   Do not reduce the new loss by the amount of the prior loss payment.
    *   Factors to consider if "reasonable steps" were taken:
        *   Time between the initial and subsequent loss.
        *   Resources available to the insured to complete repairs.
        *   Specific efforts made by the insured to mitigate damages and arrange for repairs.

*   **Scenario: Prior Loss Was Below Deductible (No Claim Made)**
    *   If an insured sustains damage but does not make a claim because the loss is below the deductible, and a subsequent event causes a larger loss, do not reduce the new loss by the amount of the prior, unclaimed damage.

#### **Roofing Products, Material Matching, & Approvals**

*   **Matching Undamaged Exterior Building Materials:**
    *   Guidelines are referenced in policy form `HO-75-19B, Claim Application - First Party Section Ill. B. Matching`. (Specific details are not provided in the images).

*   **Limited or No Availability of Roofing Products:**
    *   If damaged roofing products have limited or no availability, suitable replacement products of "like kind and quality" can be identified by:
        *   Referring to the product manufacturer or product literature.
        *   Consulting a materials expert.
        *   Ensuring compliance with applicable building codes.
    *   "Locator Services" can be used as a resource to determine product availability and identify replacements, including salvage vendors.
    *   The use of salvaged clay and concrete tiles for repairs may be considered.

*   **Approvals:**
    *   Decisions regarding replacement roofing products must be reviewed with and approved by local claim management.

#### **Building Code/Ordinance & Law**

*   If repairs or replacements require specific techniques to comply with state or local ordinances, the company's liability is limited to the portion of the loss covered by the policy contract.
*   The company will not pay for losses resulting from the enforcement of an ordinance or law unless coverage is provided under **Option OL** of the Homeowners policy.
*   **With Option OL Coverage:**
    *   If building code requires upgrades (e.g., self-adhered underlayment, ice and water barrier, drip edge/gutter apron), the claim handler should calculate the required quantities and include them in the estimate.
    *   Payment for these code-required upgrade costs should not be made until the items are actually incurred.

### 1. Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for Water Damage Claims

#### A. Tear Out Coverage (for Accessing Plumbing/Systems)
*   **Scope of Coverage:**
    *   Tear out and replacement coverage applies only to the specific part of the building that must be removed to gain access to the point where water, steam, or sewage escaped.
    *   This coverage extends to structures like driveways, sidewalks, and patios if the damage to the underlying plumbing system is from an insured loss.
*   **Exclusions:**
    *   The cost of repairing or replacing the actual system or appliance that leaked is **not** covered as part of the tear out, unless that system was damaged by a separate, covered peril.
*   **Investigative Tear Out:**
    *   Payment for "investigative tear out" (e.g., opening a concrete slab to find a leak) can be considered once a coverage decision has been made.
    *   For leaks in concrete slabs, evaluation is on a case-by-case basis. If the leak is caused by freezing, it is covered. If it is caused by gradual deterioration, it is not.

#### B. Sewer, Drain, and Sump Pump Back-up
*   **General Rule:** The standard Homeowners Policy excludes damage caused by sewer or drain back-up and sump pump overflow or failure.
*   **Distinction Between "Overflow" (Covered) vs. "Back-up" (Excluded):**
    *   **Covered Overflow:** Occurs when a blockage is located in a fixture's trap (e.g., under a sink), causing water from that fixture to spill "up and over" the drain/fixture.
    *   **Excluded Back-up:** Occurs when a blockage is located downstream in the plumbing system, causing water or sewage to enter the home *from* the drain.
*   **Sump Pumps:** Water damage caused by a failed or overflowing sump pump is not covered unless the failure was caused by a separate insured peril.
*   **Septic Systems:**
    *   Water or sewage entering the dwelling from a septic system is considered excluded surface water.
    *   Septic solids entering the home through the connected plumbing system is also a specific exclusion.
*   **Endorsements:**
    *   A "Back-up of Water or Drain" endorsement can provide coverage for losses that would otherwise be excluded.
    *   "Home Systems Protection" or "Service Line Coverage" endorsements may also provide specific coverage; refer to the relevant endorsement forms for guidance.

#### C. Other Water Damage Scenarios
*   **Surface Water:** Damage from surface water (arising from natural/external forces) is generally excluded. However, water entering the dwelling from the ground that originated from a domestic source (e.g., a broken pipe underground) is typically **not** considered surface water and may be covered, though the Sewer and Drain Back-up exclusion could still apply.
*   **Tree Roots:**
    *   Damage caused by the pressure or presence of tree roots (e.g., to a foundation or drain tile) is not covered.
    *   If a back-up is caused by tree roots and a "Back-up of Sewer or Drain" (BU/SD) endorsement is in effect, the resulting water damage is covered subject to the endorsement's limits. In this specific scenario, the cost to remove the tree roots as part of the repair is covered, but the cost to repair the damaged drain tile itself is not.
*   **Additional Living Expense (ALE):** ALE is applicable if a *covered* water loss renders the residence uninhabitable, subject to the policy and endorsement limits.

# Claims Handling Guidelines

## 1. Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for Water Damage

### A. Sewer or Drain Backup
*   **Coverage Determination:**
    *   Damage from water that backs up through a sewer or drain and overflows is covered, subject to the sub-limit of the endorsement.
    *   If surface water or ground water also enters the property, the damage caused by it is excluded. The claim handler must differentiate between the covered loss (sewer backup) and the non-covered loss (surface/ground water).
*   **Investigation:**
    *   The investigation must determine the extent of the covered sewer or drain loss versus the damage from other uncovered water sources.
*   **Coordination with National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP):**
    *   If a flood is the proximate cause of a Backup of Sewer or Drain (BOSD) loss, the NFIP policy may provide coverage.
    *   When an insured has both an NFIP policy and a State Farm BOSD endorsement, the NFIP policy is primary, and the State Farm endorsement is excess.
    *   Claim handlers must investigate and advise the policyholder to put the NFIP on notice.
*   **Additional Living Expense (ALE):**
    *   ALE may apply if the covered BOSD loss renders the home uninhabitable.

### B. Claims from Freezing
*   **General Condition for Coverage:**
    *   Damage from freezing is covered if the insured used reasonable care to maintain heat in the building at **55 degrees Fahrenheit or higher**.
*   **Frozen Pipes (Dwelling & Personal Property):**
    *   Coverage for a loss from frozen pipes is excluded unless the insured has used "reasonable care."
    *   **Definition of "Reasonable Care":**
        1.  Shut off the water supply and drain the system and appliances; **OR**
        2.  Maintain heat in the building at 55°F or higher.
    *   **Evaluation Factors:** When evaluating if reasonable care was used, consider the town, year, geography, and specific circumstances of the loss.
    *   **Covered Costs (if insured complied):**
        *   The reasonable cost incurred to thaw frozen pipes and drainage systems.
        *   The cost of excavation necessary to access the plumbing for repairs.
*   **Swimming Pools and Hot Tubs/Jacuzzi:**
    *   **Permanent Fixtures (Coverage A - Dwelling):** Coverage for freeze damage extends to the connecting filtration and circulation systems.
    *   **Portable Units (Coverage B - Personal Property):** A portable pool or jacuzzi is considered personal property and is covered for freezing.

## 2. Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for Wind & Winter Weather Damage

### A. Ice Dams
*   **Cause of Loss:** Ice dams form on the lower edge of roofs during extended sub-freezing temperatures, causing water to back up under shingles and enter the dwelling.

### B. Windstorm (Resulting in Interior Damage)
*   **Personal Property:** Damage to personal property inside a building from snow is covered **only if** a windstorm first creates an opening in the roof or a wall, allowing the snow to enter.
*   **Commercial Policies (Interior Damage):** Loss to the interior of a building from rain, snow, sleet, ice, sand, or dust (whether wind-driven or not) is **not covered unless** the building's roof or outside walls first sustain damage from a Covered Cause of Loss, which creates an opening through which these elements enter.

### C. Weight of Ice, Snow, or Sleet
*   **Free-standing Carports:**
    *   A fully enclosed and permanently affixed carport is considered a "building structure."
    *   Damage to such a structure and the personal property inside due to the weight of ice or snow would be covered.
*   **Flat Roofs:**
    *   **Investigation:** Careful inspection is required in all cases to determine the actual cause of a leak, as it may be due to deterioration rather than a covered peril. An expert may be required.
*   **Awnings:**
    *   The HW 2100 policy specifically excludes loss to awnings caused by freezing, thawing, or the pressure/weight of water, ice, or snow.
*   **Snow and Ice Removal from Roofs:**
    *   The cost of removing snow and ice from roofs and gutters is **not covered**.
    *   **Exception:** If the weight of ice/snow has already caused covered structural damage, the cost to remove the snow/ice *from the damaged area* is covered if it is necessary to facilitate repairs.

### **Wind Damage and Resulting Interior Water Damage**

*   **Coverage Trigger:** The policy covers damage to the interior of a structure or its property only if the building first sustains damage by a Covered Cause of Loss to its:
    *   Roof
    *   Outside walls
    *   Building glass (through which the rain, snow, sleet, sand, or dust enters)
*   **Ice and Snow:**
    *   Loss caused by the freezing of snow, sleet, or ice on the building or structure is covered.
    *   Interior water damage caused by the action of freezing and thawing is covered *only if* the water enters through a breach caused by an ice dam.
*   **Exclusions for Freeze Damage:**
    *   Swimming Pools and Hot Tubs/Jacuzzis are excluded from Freeze Damage coverage.
    *   The CMP 4100 policy specifically excludes swimming pools under "Section I - Property subject to Limitations".

### **Water Damage Exclusions & Exceptions (CMP 4000 Series Policies)**

This exclusion applies to Coverage A (Buildings) and Coverage B (Business Personal Property).

*   **General Exclusion Principle:** The policy does not pay for loss or damage caused directly or indirectly by water. This exclusion applies regardless of the cause of the event, whether it is gradual or sudden, widespread, or originates from natural or external forces.

*   **Definition of Excluded Water:**
    *   **Flood & Surface Water:** Flood, surface water, waves, tidal water, tsunami, and the overflow of any body of water, whether driven by wind or not.
    *   **Sewer/Drain Backup:** Water or sewage that backs up or overflows from a sewer, drain, or sump.
    *   **Ground Water:** Water below the surface of the ground that seeps or flows through foundations, walls, floors, or other openings.

*   **Ensuing Loss Coverage (Exception to the Exclusion):**
    *   The policy **will pay** for loss or damage that results from fire, explosion, or sprinkler leakage, even if that event was caused by excluded water.
    *   **Consultation Required:** If water enters a building from a source other than the surface of the ground, it may not be considered excluded "surface water." In these ambiguous cases, claim management must be consulted.

### **Sewer and Drain Back-up Guidelines**

*   **Standard Policy (CMP 4000 Series):** There is **no coverage** for damage caused by water or sewage that backs up or overflows from a sewer, drain, or sump, except when a specific endorsement is present.
    *   **Distinction:** A distinction must be made between an "accidental discharge" (e.g., a broken tap under a sink) and a "fill-up" or "overflow" (e.g., a blocked toilet overflowing). The policy is not intended to cover accidental discharge or leakage.
    *   **Coverage Condition:** For coverage to apply, water from an **external source** must fill up and enter the premises through the sewer or drain due to a blockage in the system.

*   **"Back up of Sewer or Drain" Endorsements:**
    *   These endorsements, when part of the policy, provide coverage for accidental direct physical damage to **Coverage B - Business Personal Property**.
    *   Coverage is for damage caused by water or sewage that enters or overflows from a sewer or drain located **inside the structure**.
